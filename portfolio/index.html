How Can Surface Adhesion Be Minimized?

Surface interactions play a critical role in determining the likelihood and strength of adhesion between two materials. The interactions are determined by the attractive forces (i.e. chemical interactions) that promote adhesion and the area of contact (i.e. surface area). Consequently, the surface chemistry and texture of a repellent material must be precisely controlled to minimize the adhesion of undesirable foulants.

Goal: Control and Tune the Surface Properties of Multiphase Coatings

I explored several methods to modulate the surface texture and chemistry of multiphase coatings using surface-active polymers (SAPs), including polymer formulation and external stimuli. SAPs are a class of proprietary chemicals developed at Adaptive Surface Technologies to minimize biological surface fouling.

Hull Coatings are Subject to Harse Environments

Marine coatings are exposed to various environmental forces during vessel operation, which can damage the coating and hinder its biofouling resistance. As a rule of thumb, non-biocidal marine coatings are more susceptible to damage as they use a softer polymer binder compared to biocidal marine coatings.

Goal: Develop a Durable Fouling-Release Marine Coating

I explored a combination of polymer binder chemistries (including epoxy, polyurethane, and silicone) and active ingredients to improve coating durability without compromising the biofouling performance of non-biocidal marine coatings. Two prototypes (M3 and M5) were successfully developed that met all durability and performance criteria.

Problem: Microbial Growth on 3D Printed Items

A standard FDM printer extrudes a plastic filament at high temperature through a mobile nozzle to create 3D structures. Compared to conventional plastics, the printed plastic is more susceptible to microbial retention due to an increase in surface roughness and internal void space, and not desirable for food contact applications.

Solution: Nanocomposite Ag-HDPE

My team designed a novel nanocomposite plastic to reduce microbial growth on 3D printed items. A low concentration of stabilized silver nanoparticles (AgNPs) were blended into a high-density polyethylene (HDPE) to create an antimicrobial plastic (Ag-HDPE) with thermal and mechanical stability.

Synthesis of Stabilized Silver Nanoparticles

(a) Organic synthesis of ligand-stabilized silver nanoparticles under reflux. (b) UV-Vis absorbance spectrum of the purified product confirmed the presence of silver nanoparticles.

Integration of Nanoparticles into HDPE Matrix

Silver nanoparticles were integrated into HDPE by dissolving both components in a co-solvent at high temperature. The presence of silver nanoparticles in the HDPE was confirmed via scanning electron microscopy.

Exceptional Antimicrobial Activity

HDPE and Ag-HDPE substrates were incubated with E. coli for 24 hours at room temperature. Bacterial growth was extracted from each plastic and transferred to an agar plate to count the number of viable bacterial colonies. Ag-HDPE reduced the growth of E. coli by 97% compared to a control HDPE surface.

Mechanically and Thermally Robust Plastic

The elasticity and melting temperature of Ag-HDPE were not significantly affected by the inclusion of silver nanoparticles in the plastic. Ag-HDPE has the mechanical and thermal stability of HDPE, with the added benefit of antimicrobial activity.

Bistable Liquid Crystal Displays

Bistable liquid-crystal displays (LCDs) are able to maintain a static image without using any power. When the image needs to be changed, an electrical current can be applied to the display. Combined with transparent display technology, bistable LCDs can be used to create “digital glass” displays with low power consumption.

Organic Synthesis of Liquid Crystalline Polymers

(a) Organic synthesis of smectic liquid crystalline polymers under reflux. (b) The unique structure of smectic liquid crystalline polymers was confirmed under a polarized light microscope.

Optimized Optical Formulation for Faster State Transitions and Reduced Power Consumption

Faster transitions between opaque and transparent states were achieved by optimizing the composition of the liquid crystalline solution.

Developed a Colour Formulation Using Dichroic Dye

A monochromatic colour display was developed by adding a small concentration of a compatible dichroic dye to the optimized formulation.

Improved Process for Rapid Display Prototyping

A vacuum oven and liquid crystalline dispenser were engineered to fill an LCD prototype with optical formulation ~50% faster than the previous method developed by the company.

Marine Biofouling is Pervasive on Ship Hulls

Marine organisms accumulate on ship hulls over time in a process known as biofouling, which leads to increased drag and fuel consumption during vessel operation until the hull can be cleaned. An additional challenge of biofouling is that vessels inadvertently introduce invasive species when they dislodge from the hull or are cleaned off, disrupting the local ecosystem.

Existing Products Are Toxic to the Environment

Marine hulls can be coated with a biocidal paint that slowly releases heavy metal ions, killing any attached biofouling. Not only is this method harmful to the marine environment as a whole, but it also has become less effective at preventing biofouling as some organisms have developed a resistance to the biocides.

Goal: Translate A New Technology Into A Commercial Product

I led the product development of SLIPS® Foul Protect N1, a low-friction marine paint that minimizes biofouling growth on a submerged hull without using biocides and releases any attached biofouling when the ship is in motion. The project was sponsored by ARPA-E, a subsidiary of the US Department of Energy.

SLIPS® Presents a Stable Liquid Surface to Repel Fouling

SLIPS® works by creating a stabilized liquid-infused surface. Through proper selection of the liquid and surface chemistry, the surface can be designed to repel a variety of simple and complex foulants, including marine organisms.

Enabled SLIPS® N1 Application via Roller or Spray

Scaled Up SLIPS® N1 From Lab Quantities to Gallons

Nitrogen-Doped Carbon Nanotubes

Nitrogen-doped carbon nanotubes (N-CNTs) have exceptional material properties, and have been explored to improve energy storage devices, gas sensors and catalysis. As is the case for all materials, the properties of N-CNTs depend on their structure, which is typically established during chemical synthesis.

Goal: Control N-CNT Properties via Synthesis Parameters

I explored how tuning the processing parameters during N-CNT synthesis via chemical vapour deposition may be used to control the N-CNT structure, and consequently, the physicochemical properties of N-CNTs.

Physical Structure of N-CNTs

The diameter and length of N-CNTs varied with synthesis parameters. All observed N-CNTs had a bamboo-like structure.

Thermogravimetric Analysis of N-CNTs

The thermal stability of N-CNTs increased with synthesis temperature as the higher thermal energy helped to minimize the formation of defects during N-CNT synthesis.

The Benefits of Nanoparticles for Cancer Treatment

Cancer is the one of the leading causes of death worldwide. A common strategy to treat cancer is chemotherapy, in which therapeutic drugs are used to target and destroy tumour-forming cells. The efficacy of chemotherapy can be improved by using nanoparticles to deliver the drug payload. Thanks to their small size and high surface activity, properly designed nanoparticles are capable of accumulating in tumours and providing a high drug concentration to destroy malignant cells.

Goal: Understand Nanoparticle Diffusion in Tumours

My research focused on understanding how quickly different nanoparticles will diffuse through the extracellular matrix (ECM), a fibrous network within the human body that is a major structural component of tumours.

Simulated Nanoparticle Diffusion Using An In-Vitro Model

A collagen hydrogel was used to mimic the extracellular matrix of tumour cells. One side of the prepared hydrogel slab was exposed to the nanoparticle solution in a closed container to initiate the diffusion process.

Quantified Diffusion Using Fluorescent Imaging

The diffusion coefficient of fluorescent nanoparticles into the collagen hydrogel was measured by recording the progression of the diffusion front over time using a confocal microscope.

Abstract

Liquid-infused silicones are a promising solution for common surface adhesion problems, such as ice accumulation and biofilm formation, yet they generally lack the tunability, mechanical durability and/or longevity essential for industrial applications. Self-stratifying porous silicones (SPS) infused with compatible silicone oil were developed as a passive strategy to address these shortcomings.

Preparation of Self-Stratifying Porous Silicone (SPS)

An emulsion is created by mixing water and a curable silicone resin together. (a) Water droplets near the emulsion surface evaporate and eventually disappear during the curing process, creating a non-porous skin layer. (b) Water droplets in the bulk coalesce before the polymer has fully cured, leading to a distribution of droplet sizes. Evaporation of water in the bulk continues to occur after the polymer has fully cured, creating a porous microstructure with air voids.

Tunable Porosity and Pore Size in Bulk

(a) Bulk pore density in SPS increased linearly with water concentration in the emulsion. (b) SEM images and analyzed pore size distribution of bulk porosity in SPS. The mean pore diameter (davg) and pore size distribution (σavg) increased with an increase in water concentration in the emulsion.

Protective Skin Layer Thickness at Surface

Vertical cross-sections of SPS samples confirmed the presence of a surface skin layer and the controlled thickness variation using different water concentrations.

Preparation of Liquid-Infused SPS

After curing, SPS is infused with a chemically compatible silicone oil to generate a slippery material, liquid-infused SPS (iSPS). Silicone oil was observed throughout iSPS in the bulk, with high intensity circular fluorescent domains comparably sized to pores observed in SEM images of SPS.

Excellent Fluid Repellency after Oil Infusion

Contact angle hysteresis of two immiscible liquids, water and ethanol, on SPS and PDMS before and after infusion. Sliding droplets had low hysteresis (θadv – θrec = 5° ) on iSPS after silicone oil infusion, comparable to iPDMS.

Increased Oil Capacity Compared to PDMS

(a) Infusion kinetics for SPS with different porosities immersed in silicone oil. Infusion kinetics for non-porous PDMS (iPDMS) are also shown as a comparison. At equilibrium, iSPS had more silicone oil per initial mass of dry resin compared to iPDMS, with a positive correlation between porosity and total infused oil. (i–ii) Photographs of iSPS-15 and iPDMS side profiles after infusion: iSPS maintained its original volumetric proportions while iPDMS deformed significantly during oil infusion.

Improved Oil Retention Compared to PDMS

(a) Method used to accelerate oil depletion from iSPS samples. A non-infused PDMS resin was cured and placed firmly on top of each infused sample, promoting silicone oil release from iSPS samples over time. (b) Accelerated release kinetics of silicone oil from iSPS samples. iSPS-15 retained a higher amount of oil compared to iPDMS, which resulted from a larger oil capacity and a reduced release rate after 90 h of accelerated testing.

Excellent Fouling Release After Biofilm Growth

(a) Schematic of test used to evaluate biofilm release (C. reinhardtii) from iSPS coatings under low-shear conditions. (b) Liquid-infused SPS coatings had virtually no biofilm remaining after substrates were removed from the Petri dish at low shear rate.

Strong Biofouling Prevention Vs. Model Organisms

Biological laboratory assays conducted with iSPS and control coatings using model marine fouling organisms. For comparison, the performance of a commercial standard Intersleek 900 (IS900) is denoted by a horizontal dashed line. Overall, iSPS had comparable fouling prevention to IS900 against all tested organisms.

Problem: Viscous Products In Plastic Containers

Consumer packaging containers that hold viscous liquids are common in household products, including detergents, mayonnaise and motor oil. The product’s viscosity leads to slow container drainage and residual product that sticks to the inside of the container. In addition to wasted product, the packaging requires additional cleaning before it can be recycled.

Solution: Liquid-infused Plastic Containers
